Section overview

6 sections + open response. Estimated 8–12 minutes for students. Six decisions pending from leadership (marked ◆). For the UC Berkeley Campus Climate Project report (context for peer UC framing), see references at the end of this document.

Berkeley report themes → how this draft responds

The UC Berkeley Campus Climate Project final report (UCOP) frames systemwide climate work around inclusion, safety, and the conditions under which students can learn and belong. It foregrounds experiences of exclusion or bias, institutional support and responsiveness, and the quality of disagreement and dialogue—not only counts of incidents.

What the Berkeley-style narrative tends to emphasize
  • Belonging and respect across differences
  • Fair treatment and freedom from harassment or discrimination
  • Institutional support (policies, programs, services) for diverse identities and needs
  • Expression and dialogue—whether students can participate and speak in class, with peers, and with faculty without fear
  • Reporting and follow-through—awareness of channels and perceived quality of response
Where those ideas show up in this instrument
  • Sec 1 (Q1–2): headline belonging and respect
  • Sec 2 (Q3): UCLA support for diversity by identity dimension (matrix aligned with HERI-style institutional climate)
  • Sec 3 (Q4): one topic grid; if 1 or 2, optional checkboxes for that option’s settings (D: short topic list + broader “where” list).
  • Sec 4 (Q5–6): dialogue climate and peer treatment of identity / viewpoint groups
  • Sec 5 (Q7+): experienced or observed bias or discrimination, with follow-ups
  • Sec 6 (Q10–11): self-censorship on politics and religion
  • Q12: optional open narrative
Section 3 (Expression climate / Q4) — it is a lot; how to streamline

Student preview is Option D (4 topics). A–C use one 8-topic comfort grid; a 1 or 2 triggers optional checkboxes with that version’s setting labels (no repeated matrices).

Even Option B is now 8 topic ratings plus optional checkboxes for 3 settings after any 1–2; Option A uses 5. Practical ways to lighten burden without dropping the construct:

  • Ship Option D (or C) in production for the senior survey; reserve A or B for pilots, alternate years, or deep-dive samples.
  • Fewer topic rows in production (e.g. retain race, religion, politics, and one “other sensitive” bucket) if analysts pre-specify which contrasts matter for UCLA reporting.
  • One context only for the whole class (e.g. “in class and coursework”) and move peers/faculty online to a short optional module or follow-up survey.
  • Remove student-facing A/B/C tabs in the live instrument (this draft already hides them in student preview); pick one structure after piloting.
  • Pilot for straight-lining and time-on-task; if fatigue is high, shorten before scaling.

Final wording and which streamlining steps are acceptable should be confirmed with survey methods, IRB, and any compliance or policy stakeholders who rely on specific benchmarks.

Section 3 of 6

Expression climate

How comfortable did you feel sharing your views on different topics?

A–C: one row per topic, scale 1–4 + N/A. If you choose 1 or 2 for any topic, optional checkboxes list this version’s settings. D: shorter topic list; same 1–2 rule; broader “where” checklist. All optional.

Decision needed from leadership
  1. Which version of Q4 should we adopt for production — Option D (4 topics + broad follow-up; student preview), Option C (8 topics + 2 setting choices if 1–2), Option B (8 + 3), or Option A (8 + 5) for benchmark samples?
  2. If moving beyond D, should “online” and “public campus events” stay as separate contexts, or be folded into a broader bucket?
Option D keeps the construct while avoiding grid fatigue. Options A–B mirror FIRE / Heterodox-style designs when richer topic × setting data are needed.

Choosing a form: Burden order is D → C → B → A. After any 1 or 2, optional checkboxes: A–C use that option’s own setting labels; D uses a broader place list.

8 topic ratings + optional checkboxes (2 settings) if any 1–2

Decision needed from leadership
  1. Split Q6 into Q6a (treatment of identity groups) and Q6b (treatment of expressed viewpoints), or keep as a single ambiguous item?
  2. If split, ask about treatment "by other students" only, or also "by faculty/staff"?
The current Q6 conflates identity-based and viewpoint-based treatment. Respondents will resolve the ambiguity inconsistently, which makes the data hard to interpret. Splitting adds two items to the survey but separates two distinct constructs.
Proposed Q6a + Q6b split

Treatment of identity groups

How are students from each identity group treated?

Measures: Belonging climate.
Q6b — Viewpoint climate

Treatment of expressed views

How are students treated when they express specific views (conservative, liberal, religious, critical of admin, contested issues)?

Measures: Dialogue climate.
Section 5 of 6

Bias, harassment, or discrimination

Your experiences and observations at UCLA.

7During your time at UCLA, did you personally experience or observe bias, harassment, or discrimination directed at someone because of their identity or background (such as race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, religion, disability, socioeconomic background, political beliefs, or age)?
For this question, "bias, harassment, or discrimination" refers to actions, comments, or treatment directed at someone because of their identity or background. It does not include general disagreements, dissatisfaction with academic outcomes such as grades, or interpersonal conflicts unrelated to identity.
Follow-up — shown only if you selected Experienced / Observed / Both

7a. What do you believe was the basis? (Select all that apply)

7b. Where did it primarily occur? (Select all that apply)

7c. Who was the source of the behavior? (Select all that apply)

7d. If you are comfortable, briefly describe what happened. (Optional)

If you have experienced harm and would like support, UCLA resources are available at [insert link — CARE, Office of Equity, Diversity & Inclusion, Counseling and Psychological Services].

Decision needed from leadership
  1. How detailed should Section 5 bias follow-up items be — keep the current 7a–7d structure, or expand to a HEDS / AAU-style behavioral checklist?
  2. Add a mid-survey wellness / resource-awareness check-in? (Suggested owner: Danielle)
More depth gives richer data but increases respondent burden and may broaden IRB scope. Mid-survey check-in addresses concerns about whether students know what support resources exist before they're prompted to disclose harm.

Sharing your views

How often you held back from sharing your views.

Decision needed from leadership
  1. If subgroup analysis of Q10–Q11 is required, where will political or religious background be measured (main survey module omitted here vs. admin data vs. separate instrument)?
This draft omits end-of-survey political and religious background items; headline rates on Q10–Q11 cannot be broken out by subgroup without another data source. Many external instruments pair self-censorship items with late-placement demographics to avoid priming.
